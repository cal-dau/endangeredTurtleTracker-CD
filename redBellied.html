Alabama Red-bellied Turtle

Conservation Report: The State Reptile of Alabama

1. Species Overview 2. Habitat & Range 3. Diet & Behavior 4. Nesting & Threats 5. Key Takeaway 6. Sources

Slide 1: Species Overview (The Alabama Cooter)

  • Scientific Name: *Pseudemys alabamensis* (Often confused with the northern red-bellied cooter, *P. rubriventris* is a different species).
  • Conservation Status: Federally Endangered. It has been designated the State Reptile of Alabama.
  • Size: A large freshwater turtle, with females reaching carapace lengths up to $33$ cm ($13$ inches).
  • Distinguishing Feature: A dark, slightly domed carapace (top shell) and a colorful plastron (bottom shell) ranging from yellow to orange or deep red, especially along the seams.
  • Identification: The most reliable sign is a distinct, large, arrowhead-shaped red marking on the second plastral scute (plate).

Slide 2: Habitat and Range (Mobile Bay Endemic)

  • Limited Range: This species is entirely restricted to the Mobile Bay Delta system in southern Alabama.
  • Preferred Habitat:
    • Prefers brackish and freshwater tidal areas, including tidal creeks, river channels, backwater sloughs, and swamps.
    • They need water with an abundance of aquatic vegetation, which is their main food source.
    • Crucially, they require plentiful fallen trees and floating logs for basking.
  • Salinity Tolerance: They can tolerate slightly brackish (salty) water, but generally stick to less saline areas of the delta.

Slide 3: Diet and Behavior

  • Diet: Largely **herbivorous**. Juveniles may eat some insects and small animals, but adults primarily consume aquatic and emergent vegetation, such as water hyacinth and duckweed.
  • Basking: They are **diurnal** (active during the day) and spend significant time basking. Basking is essential for regulating body temperature and producing Vitamin $D$.
  • Social Basking: It is common to see these large turtles **stacked on top of each other** on preferred basking logs, often with other turtle species.
  • Hiding: When startled, they quickly slide off their basking sites into the water, hiding among submerged vegetation or debris.

Slide 4: Nesting and Threats

Visual: Coastal River System

  • Nesting Period: Females leave the water to nest from late April to July, usually on sandy beaches or high banks close to the water.
  • Clutch Size: They lay an average of $6$ to $10$ eggs per clutch and may lay several clutches per season.
  • Primary Threat: **Habitat Loss and Water Quality** due to urbanization and pollution impacting the Mobile Bay system.
  • Secondary Threat (Boat Mortality): Their habit of basking on floating objects in river channels makes them highly vulnerable to collisions with boats and barges.
  • Predation: Nests are highly vulnerable to predation by raccoons and other mammals.

Slide 5: Key Takeaway: Fragility of Endemic Species

★ Extreme Vulnerability: The Alabama Red-bellied Turtle exists in only one river delta system in the world, making its entire population highly susceptible to local environmental disasters (like a single large chemical spill).

★ Conservation Goal: The focus is on protecting the remaining nesting sites and ensuring the water quality of the Mobile Bay Delta, which is an ecologically important but heavily developed area.

★ State Symbolism: Its designation as the State Reptile increases public awareness, but active habitat management remains the most critical need for its survival.
