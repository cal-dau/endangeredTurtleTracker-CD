Flattened Musk Turtle

Conservation Report: Rare Dweller of the Alabama Streams

1. Species Overview 2. Location & Habitat 3. Unique Behavior 4. Shell Morphology 5. Key Takeaway 6. Sources

Slide 1: Species Overview (The Stream Specialist)

  • Scientific Name: *Sternotherus depressus*
  • Conservation Status: Federally Threatened. It is one of the most imperiled turtles in North America.
  • Size: Very small, with a carapace length rarely exceeding $11.5$ cm ($4.5$ inches).
  • Distinguishing Feature: Its shell is noticeably flatter and more oval than other musk turtles, an adaptation for living under rocks and crevices in fast-moving streams.
  • "Stinkpot": All musk turtles, including this one, can release a foul-smelling musk when handled, used as a defense mechanism.

Visual: Flattened Musk Turtle in Water

Slide 2: Location and Habitat (Endemic Range)

  • Limited Range: This turtle is endemic (naturally found only) to a highly restricted area in the **Mobile Basin** in northern Alabama, USA.
  • Specific Habitat:
    • Prefers clear, rocky streams and medium-sized rivers with moderate currents.
    • They live primarily on the river bottom, seeking shelter among limestone crevices, slab rocks, and submerged logs.
  • Activity: Mostly aquatic and nocturnal, meaning they are active at night underwater.
  • Diet: Carnivorous. They mainly eat aquatic insects (like caddisfly larvae), snails, mussels, and small crayfish.

Slide 3: Unique Behavior

  • Aestivation: Unlike hibernation, these turtles often enter a period of **aestivation** (dormancy during the summer) when stream conditions become dry or stagnant.
  • Nesting: Females lay a very small clutch of eggs, typically only 1 to 3, usually under rocks or in sandy stream banks near the water's edge. They may lay multiple clutches per year.
  • Foraging: They are **"bottom-walkers,"** moving along the stream bed to find food instead of swimming freely.
  • Sexual Maturity: They are slow to mature, which makes it harder for the species to recover from population losses. Females may not reach maturity until $7$ to $10$ years of age.

Slide 4: Shell Morphology (An Adaptation)

Visual: Flattened Shell Profile

  • Carapace Shape: The extremely low, flat shape of the shell is its defining feature.
  • Adaptive Advantage:
    • Allows the turtle to crawl under rocks and wedge itself into tight crevices to avoid the current and predators.
    • Provides excellent camouflage against the flat, rocky substrate of the river bottom.
  • Plastron: The plastron (bottom shell) is small, allowing for greater limb movement for gripping rocks and climbing.
  • Algae Growth: Shells often have dense algae growth, further aiding in camouflage.

Slide 5: Key Takeaway: Habitat Isolation and Pollution

★ Restricted Range: Because it lives only in a very small area of Alabama, any significant local event (like a major pollution spill or dam construction) can wipe out a huge part of the entire global population.

★ Water Quality: It requires high-quality, clean, flowing water. Runoff from agriculture, mining, or sewage pollution is extremely harmful to the turtle and its invertebrate food sources.

★ Conservation Focus: Protection involves monitoring water quality, maintaining natural stream flow, and enforcing regulations to prevent pollution in its small, critical habitat.
