Kemp's Ridley Sea Turtle

Conservation Report: Endangered Giant of the Gulf

1. Species Overview 2. Location & Habitat 3. Nesting Habits 4. Locomotion & Tracks 5. Key Takeaway 6. Sources

Slide 1: Species Overview (The Smallest Sea Turtle)

  • Scientific Name: *Lepidochelys kempii*
  • Conservation Status: Critically Endangered
  • Size: The smallest sea turtle species, typically measuring $60 - 70$ cm ($24 - 28$ inches) and weighing about $35 - 45$ kg ($75 - 100$ lbs).
  • Distinguishing Feature: Dark gray or olive-green carapace (shell) with a creamy white or yellowish plastron (bottom shell). Its powerful jaws are adapted for crushing hard-shelled prey.

Visual: Adult Kemp's Ridley Swimming

Slide 2: Location and Habitat

  • Primary Range: The entire Gulf of Mexico is the main foraging and breeding ground for adults.
  • Juvenile Range: Young turtles travel widely along the U.S. Atlantic coast, found from Florida all the way up to New England and occasionally Nova Scotia.
  • Preferred Habitat (Adults):
    • Shallow Waters: They are "neritic" species, staying in coastal, shallow waters (rarely venturing deeper than $50$ meters).
    • Bottom Type: They prefer muddy or sandy bottoms, often near estuaries and shallow seagrass beds.
  • Diet: They are carnivorous benthic (sea floor) feeders, primarily eating crabs, but also mollusks, shrimp, and occasional fish or jellyfish.

Slide 3: Unique Nesting Habits (The Arribada)

  • Primary Nesting Site: Almost 95% of worldwide nesting occurs on a single stretch of beach in Tamaulipas, Mexico, particularly at Rancho Nuevo.
  • Timing: Nesting occurs between April and July. Females typically nest 2 to 3 times per season.
  • Diurnal Nesting: They are one of the only sea turtle species that nests primarily during daylight hours.
  • The "Arribada" (Arrival): They are famous for their synchronized mass nesting events, called *arribadas* (Spanish for "arrival"). Hundreds or even thousands of females emerge from the sea together.
  • Clutch: Each nest (clutch) contains approximately $100 - 110$ eggs, which incubate for about $50 - 60$ days.

Slide 4: Locomotion and Tracks

Visual: Tracks in the Sand (Crawl Marks)

  • Ocean Locomotion: They use their front flippers for powerful propulsion and their smaller rear flippers for steering.
  • Beach Locomotion (Crawl Pattern):
    • Kemp’s Ridleys use an alternating crawl motion (one front flipper and the opposite rear flipper move together).
  • Tracks (Crawl Marks):
    • Alternating Scoops: The track shows comma-shaped scoop marks alternating on both sides.
    • Central Drag Mark: A narrow, continuous or broken straight line dragging down the center from the tail/plastron.
    • Width: Average crawl width is about $80$ cm ($31$ inches).

Slide 5: Key Takeaway: Vulnerability & Conservation

★ Rarest Turtle: The Kemp's Ridley is considered the most endangered sea turtle globally.

★ Concentrated Vulnerability: Their reliance on one primary mass nesting beach (*arribada* site in Mexico) makes the entire population extremely vulnerable to a single disaster (like an oil spill) or habitat destruction.

★ Conservation Success (Partial): Intensive efforts, including protecting the nesting beaches and requiring Turtle Excluder Devices (TEDs) in fishing nets, have helped the population rebound from its historic lows.
