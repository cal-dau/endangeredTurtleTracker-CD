Loggerhead Sea Turtle

Conservation Report: Global Traveler and Coastal Forager

1. Species Overview 2. Location & Habitat 3. Nesting Habits 4. Locomotion & Tracks 5. Key Takeaway 6. Sources

Slide 1: Species Overview (The Coastal Forager)

  • Scientific Name: *Caretta caretta*
  • Conservation Status: Endangered (Globally, with varying population segments).
  • Size: A large species, typically measuring $90 - 110$ cm ($35 - 43$ inches) in carapace length and weighing about $100 - 160$ kg ($220 - 350$ lbs).
  • Distinguishing Feature: Named for its very large head and powerful jaw muscles. The carapace (shell) is characteristically reddish-brown.

Visual: Adult Loggerhead Swimming

Slide 2: Location and Habitat

  • Global Range: Found in temperate and tropical regions across the Atlantic, Pacific, and Indian Oceans. They have a truly global distribution.
  • Major Populations: Key areas include the Southeast U.S. coast, Mediterranean Sea, Pacific Mexico, and Australia.
  • Preferred Habitat:
    • Diverse: Found in coastal shallow waters, estuaries, and deeper open ocean habitats during migration.
    • Bottom Type: Favors hard-bottom habitats (rocky reefs) and soft-bottom areas (sand, mud) where hard-shelled prey live.
  • Diet: Benthic (sea floor) carnivore. The primary diet consists of hard-shelled invertebrates like conchs, crabs, clams, and sea urchins.

Slide 3: Unique Nesting Habits

  • Primary Nesting Sites: The single largest nesting population is found in Florida, USA. Other major sites are in Oman, Australia, and Brazil.
  • Timing: Nesting occurs from late spring to early fall (May through August).
  • No Arribadas: Unlike the Kemp's Ridley, the Loggerhead is a solitary nester, emerging alone to lay eggs.
  • Nocturnal Nesting: They nest primarily during nighttime hours, using the dark for cover.
  • Clutch: Females nest every 2 to 4 years, laying about 3 to 5 clutches (nests) per season, with an average of $100$ to $126$ eggs per nest. Incubation lasts around $60$ days.

Slide 4: Locomotion and Tracks

Visual: Tracks in the Sand (Crawl Marks)

  • Ocean Locomotion: Powerful swimmers, capable of long-distance migrations between feeding and nesting grounds.
  • Beach Locomotion (Crawl Pattern):
    • Loggerheads use a symmetrical crawl motion (both front flippers move together, followed by both rear flippers).
  • Tracks (Crawl Marks):
    • Parallel Trenches: The track shows deep, distinct parallel lines or scoops created by the flippers moving simultaneously.
    • No Central Drag Mark: Typically, there is no noticeable central drag mark (unlike the Kemp's Ridley).
    • Width: Average crawl width is about $70 - 100$ cm ($27 - 40$ inches), making them one of the widest tracks.

Slide 5: Key Takeaway: Widespread Threat

★ Bycatch Vulnerability: Because they frequent coastal areas and continental shelves, Loggerheads are highly susceptible to accidental capture (bycatch) in commercial fishing gear, particularly trawls and longlines.

★ Hatchling Disorientation: Their nocturnal nesting exposes hatchlings to artificial lighting on beaches, causing them to crawl away from the ocean and die from exhaustion or predators.

★ Conservation Focus: Protection involves managing fisheries, preserving crucial nesting beaches (especially in Florida), and efforts to reduce plastic pollution that they often mistake for food.
